Vigenère

A keyword picks a different Caesar shift for each successive letter, so one plaintext letter becomes many ciphertext letters and the frequency histogram flattens. For three centuries this was called le chiffre indéchiffrable. Then someone noticed the keyword repeats.

Teaching cipher. Vigenère falls to a pencil, some patience, and the two tests below. Never use it for anything real.
How it works

A rolling key

Write the keyword under the message, repeating it. Each plaintext letter is Caesar-shifted by the letter above it. A=shift 0, B=shift 1, and so on.

Ciphertext

Same plaintext letter, different output — the single-letter frequencies smear out. That is why the substitution attack stalls here.

How it breaks

Find the period, and it collapses into Caesars

The keyword's length is the whole game. If it is k, then letters 1, 1+k, 1+2k… were all shifted by the same keyword letter — so they form a single Caesar cipher. Find k and Vigenère falls apart into k easy problems. Two tests point at k:

Test 1 · Friedman (index of coincidence)

For each guessed period, split into that many columns and average their coincidence rate. English ≈ 0.067, random ≈ 0.038. A period whose bar reaches toward 0.067 is likely right.

Test 2 · Kasiski (repeated sequences)

Repeats in the ciphertext are usually the same word hitting the same slice of key. The gaps between them are multiples of k. Which factor keeps recurring?

What it forced

What it forced

Vigenère leaks because the key repeats, creating a period to find. Kill the repetition and you kill the attack: use a key as long as the message and never repeated. Take that idea to its limit — a truly random key, used once — and you arrive at the only cipher that is provably unbreakable: the one-time pad.
