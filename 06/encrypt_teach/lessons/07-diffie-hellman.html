Stop 7

Diffie–Hellman

Symmetric crypto is solved — if both sides already share a key. But how do two strangers agree on a secret while an eavesdropper records every word? In 1976 the impossible turned out to be easy: mix public values so that only the two participants can compute the shared result. Its security rests on a one-way street called the discrete logarithm.

Toy primes. The exchange below uses an ~11-bit prime so the attack runs instantly. Real deployments use a prime of at least 2048 bits, or an elliptic curve such as X25519. The genuine Web-Crypto version is shown at the bottom.
How it works

Public mixing, private result

Everyone agrees on a prime p and base g (public). Alice picks a secret a, Bob picks a secret b. They exchange ga and gb in the open. Each raises the other's value to their own secret, and both land on gab — a number nobody on the wire can compute.

How it breaks

Two attackers, two very different outcomes

Eve, listening (passive)

Eve records p, g, ga, gb. To get the secret she must invert the exponentiation — the discrete log. With a real 2048-bit prime that is infeasible. With our toy prime, she just tries every exponent:

This does not mean DH is weak — it means the prime must be big. Passive eavesdropping is defeated by size alone.

Mallory, in the middle (active)

Now the real problem. Mallory doesn't listen — she intercepts. She runs one DH exchange with Alice and a separate one with Bob, pretending to each that she is the other. Both think they're secure; Mallory holds both keys and relays (reading, editing) everything. No amount of prime-size helps, because the math never told anyone who was on the far end.

Diffie–Hellman defeats the eavesdropper but not the impostor. Secrecy is not enough; you need to bind a key to an identity — a way to prove “this public value really is Bob's.” That demands a trapdoor tied to a secret only Bob holds, usable for signing and for encrypting to him. Enter the most famous public-key system of all: RSA.
