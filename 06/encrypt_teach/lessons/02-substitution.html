Keyed substitution

Caesar died of a tiny keyspace, so we make the keyspace astronomical: the key is now a full scramble of the alphabet, one of 26! ≈ 4×10²⁶ ≈ 2⁸⁸ orderings. Brute force is finished — trying every key would outlast the universe. And it does not matter in the slightest.

Teaching cipher. A monoalphabetic substitution is broken by a schoolchild with a pencil. It is here to make one point unforgettable. Never use it for anything real.
How it works

Every letter gets a fixed stand-in

The key maps A→? , B→? , … , Z→? — a fixed, one-to-one substitution. Same plaintext letter, same ciphertext letter, every time. That consistency is exactly the leak.

Frequency analysis — you crack it, not the computer

The cipher hid which letter is which, but not how often each appears. English leans hard on E, T, A, O, N; it barely touches Q, X, Z. That fingerprint survives substitution untouched. Below is an intercepted message. Its letter histogram is drawn against the English baseline — the shapes rhyme.

Your decryption

The lesson. You just searched a space of 2⁸⁸ keys without trying a single key. Keyspace size is not security. A cipher leaks through any structure it fails to destroy — here, letter frequencies. Size bought nothing.

What it forced

What it forced

To survive frequency analysis, a cipher must stop mapping each letter the same way every time. The answer: use many substitution alphabets and rotate between them, so one plaintext letter becomes many different ciphertext letters and the tell-tale histogram flattens. That is the polyalphabetic cipher — next, Vigenère.
