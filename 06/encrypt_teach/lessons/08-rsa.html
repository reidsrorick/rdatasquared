Stop 8

RSA

Diffie–Hellman needed a way to tie a key to an identity. RSA delivers it with a trapdoor permutation: a function anyone can compute forwards (encrypt with the public key) but only the holder of the secret factors can invert (decrypt with the private key). It works. And the “obvious” way to use it hides a flaw that forced the whole field to define what “secure” even means.

Toy primes. The keys below are ~27-bit. Real RSA uses a modulus of at least 2048 bits (primes ~1024 bits) with randomized padding (OAEP). This code is textbook RSA for teaching — deterministic and unpadded on purpose. Never use it for anything real.
How it works

One-way, unless you know the factors

Pick two primes p, q; their product n is public. Encryption is c = me mod n; decryption is m = cd mod n, where d is computable only from p and q. Factoring n back into p·q is the hard problem that protects d.

How it breaks

Textbook RSA is deterministic — so it leaks

Notice something about the encryption above: the same m always gives the same c. There is no randomness. That feels harmless until the attacker knows your message comes from a small set of possibilities — a yes/no, a price, a command. Then determinism is fatal: the attacker simply encrypts every possibility with your public key and looks for a match. No private key. No factoring.

What it forced

Why “secure” needs an adversary, not a vibe

Textbook RSA is a perfectly good trapdoor and a broken encryption scheme. The gap is that “hard to invert” is not the same as “leaks nothing.” To rule out attacks like the one you just ran, cryptographers stopped arguing from intuition and defined security as a game: IND-CPA — an adversary who picks two messages and gets one encrypted must not guess which, better than a coin flip. Deterministic encryption loses that game instantly (encrypt both candidates yourself and compare). The fix — randomized padding — is exactly what makes RSA win it. This is the moment cryptography became a science with definitions instead of a bag of tricks. The last stop asks whether the implementations of all this theory actually hold up.
