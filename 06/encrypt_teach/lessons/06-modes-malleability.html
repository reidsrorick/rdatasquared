Modes & malleability

CTR mode fixes ECB by turning the block cipher into a keystream generator: C = P ⊕ keystream. The ciphertext is now unpredictable — but it is also editable. Confusing “the attacker can't read it” with “the attacker can't change it” is one of the most expensive mistakes in the field.

How it works

CTR is a pad you can regenerate

AES encrypts a running counter to produce a keystream, which is XORed with the plaintext — exactly the one-time-pad operation, but the pad comes from a short key you can reuse safely (with a fresh nonce each time). This hides content well. Notice, though, that XOR is linear: the relationship between ciphertext and plaintext is a simple, attacker-controllable one.

How it breaks

Bit-flipping: rewrite the message without the key

Because C = P ⊕ keystream, flipping ciphertext bit i flips plaintext bit i when the receiver decrypts — and touches nothing else. The keystream is unknown to you and it does not matter. Here is an access token encrypted with real AES-CTR. You are on the wire with the ciphertext and no key. Turn admin=0 into admin=1.

The lesson. Encryption is not authentication. Secrecy hid the message; it did nothing to prove the message arrived unaltered. A confidential channel with no integrity is a channel an attacker can forge on.

What it forced

AEAD — encrypt and authenticate

The fix is to attach a cryptographic tag that verifies on decryption, so any tampering is rejected instead of silently decrypted. Tampering no longer edits the message — it destroys it, and the receiver knows. With confidentiality and integrity finally in one box, symmetric cryptography is essentially solved. One enormous problem remains, and it is not about hiding messages at all: how do two people who have never met agree on a secret key in the first place? That question opens the asymmetric world — Diffie–Hellman is next.
