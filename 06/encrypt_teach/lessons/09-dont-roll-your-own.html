Stop 9 · The real world

Don't roll your own — the mechanism

“Don't roll your own crypto” is repeated so often it has stopped meaning anything. So forget the commandment. Here are two systems built from correct primitives — a real MAC, a real cipher — that leak everything anyway, because of how they were implemented. You will run both attacks yourself. The reason is the lesson.

How it works

The primitives are fine

HMAC-SHA-256 is an excellent way to authenticate a message: attach a tag, and the receiver recomputes it and checks for equality. AES is an excellent cipher. Neither is what breaks below. What breaks is the equality check and the randomness source — the unglamorous plumbing around the math.

How it breaks

Break 1 — a timing side channel on the tag check

A common, natural, wrong way to verify a tag is if (provided === real) — a compare that stops at the first wrong byte. That means a tag with a correct first byte takes infinitesimally longer to reject than one that's wrong immediately. Do that measurement enough times and the secret tag falls out one byte at a time — the attacker never sees it. One honest wrinkle: the last byte leaves no timing trace (matching or missing it, the compare ends at the same length), so the attack times every byte but the last, then brute-forces that final byte against the plain accept/reject answer. Below, a secret tag is checked by exactly such a compare. The chart times all 256 values for the current byte position; the tallest bar is the correct byte. (The work auto-calibrates to your machine, so a full recovery takes a handful of seconds.)

Break 2 — a weak RNG makes predictable keys

Every key on this whole site assumed one thing: real randomness. Seed your generator from something guessable — say, the current time in seconds, a genuine historical bug — and your “random” key lives in a space an attacker can search in a blink. Two keys made in the same second are identical.

What it forced

What it forced

Neither attack touched the mathematics. They exploited a comparison that returned too early and a generator seeded from something guessable. This is the real content of “don't roll your own”: correctness of a cryptosystem lives in constant-time code, uniform randomness, correct nonces, padding, and a hundred other details that vetted libraries have already gotten wrong once and fixed. Use AES-GCM, HMAC, X25519 from an audited implementation — not because a rule says so, but because you have now watched what the plumbing costs when it leaks.
