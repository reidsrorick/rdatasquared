Stop 1

Caesar

Every letter slides the same number of places along the alphabet. The entire secret — the key — is that one number, from 1 to 25. A cipher is only as strong as the effort needed to try every key, and here that effort is: try 25 things.

Teaching cipher. Caesar has no security. It is here to establish what a key is, and what “too small” means. Never use it for anything real.

What it forced

What it forced

The fix looks obvious: make the key enormous. Instead of shifting the whole alphabet by one number, scramble it into any of its 26! ≈ 2⁸⁸ possible orderings. That is the substitution cipher — and the next stop is where we discover that a titanic keyspace saves you from brute force and from nothing else.
